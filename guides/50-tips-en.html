Beginner's Photography Guide

50 Tips to Make You
a Better Photographer

From the basic concepts to the secrets of composition — everything you need to take photos you're proud of.

Amit Erez
amitphotos.com

A Word Before We Begin

I've been shooting for many years — with a Nikon D300, D700, and now the Z7. Along the way I learned a lot, mostly from mistakes. This guide is everything I wish someone had told me at the beginning.

These 50 tips don't require prior knowledge or expensive gear. They only require one thing: that you go out and shoot.

Every tip here is based on real field experience — not theory. Some will make sense immediately, others will only click after you try them. Keep this guide, and come back to it when you're stuck.

Shoot a lot. Fail. Try again. That's the whole secret.

— Amit Erez
Part One · Tips 1–8

Know Your Camera

Before heading out to shoot — you need to understand what you're working with. It's not painful, I promise.

1
Read the Manual — At Least Once
Most people never open it. Those who do discover features they had no idea existed. Two hours with the manual is worth dozens of hours of trial and error.
2
Get Off Auto — Starting Tomorrow Morning
Aperture Priority (Av or A) is the first step. You control the aperture — the camera calculates the rest. Wide aperture (f/1.8) = bokeh. Narrow aperture (f/11) = everything sharp.
3
Shoot RAW, Not JPEG
JPEG is a photo that has already been processed and compressed. RAW contains all the data your sensor captured — flexible for correction afterward. Slightly off exposure? Fixable in RAW. In JPEG — not always.
4
Three Controls That Run Everything
ISO (light sensitivity) · Aperture (how much light enters) · Shutter Speed (how long light enters). All other controls are details. First — master these three.
The Golden Triangle: Changing ISO affects noise. Changing aperture affects bokeh and depth of field. Changing shutter speed affects motion. When you change one — you need to compensate with one of the other two.
5
The Histogram Doesn't Lie — The Screen Does
Your camera's screen is a liar. In bright sun it looks too dark. At night it looks too bright. The histogram is a real graph of exposure. Left = dark, Right = bright. Clipped right edge = lost highlight detail.
6
Focus Lock — The Secret to Sharp Photos
Half-press the shutter to focus → reframe → full press. This lets you place the focus point exactly where you want it, not just in the center of the frame.
7
Three Metering Modes — Know When to Use Each
Matrix/Evaluative: Meters the whole frame — good for most situations. Center-Weighted: Emphasis on the center — for portraits. Spot: Measures a single point — for extreme lighting situations.
8
Clean Your Sensor — One Dust Spot Ruins a Thousand Photos
Dust spots show clearly on clear skies and white walls. Test by shooting a clear blue sky at f/16 — every spot will appear clearly. Sensor cleaning is simple and inexpensive.

Part Two · Tips 9–16

Nailing Exposure

Exposure isn't just "bright or dark" — it's complete control over how much light enters your photo.

9
Getting Exposure Right in the Field Beats Fixing in Post
Post-processing is a tool — not magic. A correctly exposed photo preserves detail in both shadows and highlights. A blown photo has no highlight detail, no matter how far you push the slider.
10
ETTR — Expose to the Right
Expose as brightly as possible without blowing highlights. The histogram should approach the right edge without touching it. This reduces noise and preserves shadow detail.
11
ISO — Always Start from the Lowest Setting
High ISO = high light sensitivity = more noise. ISO 100–400: clean. ISO 800–1600: acceptable. ISO 3200+: noisy (camera-dependent). Always start at ISO 100 and go up only when necessary.
12
The Shutter Speed Rule of Thumb — Prevent Camera Shake
Shutter speed no slower than 1/focal length. With a 50mm lens → minimum 1/60s. With 200mm → minimum 1/200s. With image stabilization — you can go 2–4 stops below that.
Quick tip: Auto-ISO with a manual ceiling (e.g., ISO 3200) + minimum shutter speed (e.g., 1/125s) = the camera manages ISO automatically while maintaining a shake-free shutter speed. A game-changing setting.
13
Auto-ISO with a Manual Ceiling — The Setting That Changes Everything
Set Auto-ISO with a minimum shutter speed in your camera settings. The camera raises ISO only when needed to maintain shutter speed. Perfect for sports and events.
14
ND Filter — Long Exposures in Daylight
ND darkens light without changing color. ND10 = 10 stops of light = seconds-long exposure in bright daylight. Flowing water becomes silky white. The sea becomes a mirror. Clouds become soft fabric.
15
Exposure Compensation (+/−) — The Most Useful Tool
The +/- button adds or subtracts exposure without changing mode. Snow? +1 stop. Dark subject against bright background? -1 stop. Most cameras give you quick access to this button — use it constantly.
16
Backlit Subjects — Meter on the Face, Not the Background
When the background is brighter than the subject, the camera exposes for the background — and your subject comes out dark. Fix: Spot Metering on the face, or Exposure Compensation +1 to +2. Alternative: fill flash.
Part Three · Tips 17–24

Composition

What separates an interesting photo from a snapshot — it's not the gear, it's the choice of what enters the frame.

17
Rule of Thirds — Place Your Subject at the Intersections
Imagine two vertical and two horizontal lines dividing the frame into nine equal parts. Place your main subject at one of the four intersection points — not the center. Most cameras support displaying this grid in live view.
18
Leading Lines — Invite the Eye to Travel Through the Frame
Roads, fences, rivers, train tracks — any line that starts at the edge of the frame and leads to the subject. This creates depth and draws the viewer in. Look for them — they're everywhere.
19
Framing — Place Your Subject Inside a Natural Frame
Trees, arches, doorways, windows — any element that surrounds the subject creates a natural frame that concentrates attention. It also adds a layer of depth to a two-dimensional image.
20
Simplicity — Remove Everything That Doesn't Contribute
Ask yourself: "Does every element in this frame strengthen the story, or distract from it?" A step forward, a different angle, a wider aperture — sometimes all you need is to remove the visual noise.
White owl on white snow = clean background that emphasizes the subject. Simplicity in composition.
21
Change Your Height — Don't Always Shoot at Eye Level
From below: the subject looks large and powerful. From above: gives a sense of control and perspective. Simply lying on the ground or standing on a bench changes everything.
22
Breathing Room — Give Your Subject Space
If the subject is looking left — give it space on the left. A bird in flight needs room in the direction it's flying. A frame where the subject feels "squeezed" is uncomfortable for the viewer.
23
Horizontal vs Vertical — Try Both
Before moving on — shoot both orientations. Landscapes are almost always horizontal. Portraits mostly vertical. Architecture — it depends. But sometimes the unexpected orientation works better.
24
Negative Space — The Empty Area Is Part of the Photo
A small figure in a vast sky creates a sense of solitude and beauty. Sometimes what isn't in the photo tells the story. Don't be afraid of empty, clean backgrounds.
Part Four · Tips 25–31

Understanding Light

Photography is painting with light — whoever controls the light, controls the image.

25
Golden Hour — Worth More Than Any Gear
One hour after sunrise and one hour before sunset: the light is warm, soft, and low. Skin in golden hour looks perfect. Landscape in golden hour looks magical. No filter replicates it. Wake up early.
26
Blue Hour — The Photographers' Secret
Twenty minutes before sunrise and after sunset: the sky turns into a deep, gentle, even blue. City lights on, deep blue sky — a combination you can't miss. This is when to shoot cities at night.
27
Overcast Day Is a Gift for Portraits
Clouds diffuse sunlight into soft, ambient illumination — a giant free softbox. No harsh shadows, no squinting eyes, no extreme contrast. Perfect for portraits and macro.
28
Light Direction — Three Completely Different Effects
Front light: Flat, no shadows, bright faces. Side light: Dramatic, emphasizes textures. Back light (rim light): Mystical, separates subject from background. Try rotating the subject relative to the light — don't move yourself.
Quick tip: A piece of white cardboard reflects light onto faces in shadow. A professional reflector does the same thing — just more convenient to carry. Before buying one — try cardboard.
29
Reflector — The Cheapest Gear with the Most Impact
A 5-in-1 reflector costs almost nothing and transforms every portrait. Silver = sharp and contrasty. Gold = warm and soft. White = subtle and natural. I always have one in my bag.
30
Flash in Daylight — Not Just for Night
When strong sun is behind the subject, fill flash adds light to faces. Low power (-1 to -2 stops) = completely natural. Nobody will know you used flash.
31
Look for Special Light — It's Waiting for You
A shaft of light through a window, rays between trees, the opening of a tunnel, neon lights in rain. The most dramatic light is usually free — you just need to notice it and wait.
Part Five · Tips 32–38

Lenses & Gear

Gear is a tool — not the story. But a good tool helps.

32
A Good Lens Beats an Expensive Camera Body
An f/1.4 lens on an old body will beat an expensive body with a kit lens every time. Limited budget? Buy a modest body + a good prime lens. Optical glass doesn't age like a camera body.
33
50mm f/1.8 — The First Lens You Should Buy
Inexpensive ($100–200), light, sharp, with beautiful bokeh and a wide aperture for low light. The "Nifty Fifty" — a nickname that fits perfectly. The angle is close to the human eye = photos that feel natural.
34
Tripod — Not Optional, Mandatory
Long exposures, sunrise landscapes, macro, night — impossible without a tripod. Carbon fiber: light and stable. Worth investing in — a good tripod lasts twenty years.
35
UV Filter — Cheap Insurance for Expensive Glass
Protects the front element from scratches, fingerprints and moisture. A $2,000 lens + a $30 UV filter = armor. Always on every lens. Never removed.
36
Less Gear = More Creativity
Don't take everything everywhere. Constraints force creative solutions. One day with a single lens teaches more about photography than a week with the whole bag.
37
Teleconverter — Extend Your Reach Without Buying a New Lens
A 1.4× TC on a 300mm lens = 420mm. A 2× TC = 600mm. Much less expensive than a new telephoto lens. Cost: you lose light (1.4× = one stop, 2× = two stops).
38
Lens Cleaning — Only This Way, No Other
Gentle breath to lift dust + clean microfiber cloth, gentle circular wipe. Tissue, your shirt, paper towels — forbidden. They scratch the coating. Dedicated lens cleaning solution — allowed.
Part Six · Tips 39–44

Editing

Good editing brings a photo to its potential — it doesn't rescue it.

39
Editing Enhances — It Doesn't Save
A photo with composition problems, bad focus or extreme exposure won't become a good photo in editing. Rule one: shoot well in the field. Editing is the finishing touch.
40
Always Start with Exposure and White Balance
Before anything else — fix the basic exposure and White Balance. Every subsequent adjustment to color, contrast and detail will be far more accurate once the foundation is right.
41
Less Saturation — Almost Always
Beginners overdo color. Saturation of 10–20 is enough for most photos. To check: convert to black and white, then restore color — if the color jumps out at you, you've overdone it.
42
Clarity — Use It Carefully
Clarity adds sharpness to mid-range textures. Excellent for landscapes, rocks, wood. Destructive for portraits — turns skin into a lunar landscape. If you shot faces — Clarity at 0 or slightly negative.
43
Save a Preset — Save Hours
When you find a style you love — save it as a Preset in Lightroom. Next time: select, click, done. Consistent editing also builds a recognizable "signature style".
44
Compare Before and After — Sometimes the Original Is Better
After finishing your edit — press \ in Lightroom to see before and after. Sometimes thirty minutes of editing ruined a photo that was perfect. There's always undo.
Part Seven · Tips 45–50

Mindset & Practice

The best gear in the world doesn't shoot for you. Habits do.

45
Shoot Every Day — Even with Your Phone
Your eye trains itself through shooting. Someone who shoots daily for a year takes far better photos than someone who goes out with expensive gear once a month. The phone in your pocket is the best camera you have — because it's always with you.
46
Learn from Photos You Love — Analyze Them
When you see an impressive photo — stop and ask: Where's the light coming from? What's the composition? What lens? What made me stop? Analyzing five photos a day is worth a professional lesson.
47
Accept Critique — It Doesn't Hurt as Much as You Think
Show your work to people you respect and sincerely ask what isn't working. One honest critique is worth a hundred "great shots!" from people who don't want to hurt your feelings.
48
Fewer Shots = More Thinking
Digital cameras tempt us to shoot hundreds of frames hoping one will be good. Try the opposite: stand before your subject, think about the frame, then shoot — once. The improvement is immediate.
49
Fall in Love with Your Subject
The best photographers shoot what they love — birds, landscapes, children, food. Love for the subject creates patience, persistence and attention to detail that can't be faked.
50
Most Important — Go Out and Shoot
All the knowledge in the world is worthless without going out to the field. The best camera is the one you have in your hand. The best gear is the gear you actually take with you. Now — go shoot.
